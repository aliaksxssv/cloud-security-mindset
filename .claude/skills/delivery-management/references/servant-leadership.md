# Servant Leadership

The servant leader inverts the pyramid: the leader exists to remove obstacles, grow people, and enable outcomes — not to command. Complex work (see `triangle.md`, Cynefin) cannot be managed by directive control; it needs teams that think, and teams think when they are served, not supervised.

## The ten attributes (Greenleaf / Spears)

1. **Listening** — seek first to understand: listen receptively to what is said and *not* said, before advocating a position. The default response to a problem brought to you is a question, not a solution.
2. **Empathy** — assume good intentions; strive to understand and share the other person's perspective, especially when rejecting their idea or work. People need to be accepted and recognized for their unique spirit.
3. **Healing** — repair relationships and restore people after failure, conflict, or burnout. A team that fears blame hides problems; a healed team surfaces them early.
4. **Self-awareness** — know your own strengths, biases, triggers, and the effect you have on the room. Your mood and your reactions are amplified by your role.
5. **Persuasion** — build conviction rather than compel compliance; rely on arguments and shared goals, not positional authority. Consensus built by persuasion outlasts orders.
6. **Conceptualization** — think beyond day-to-day operations; hold and communicate the bigger picture ("dream great dreams") so the team can make aligned decisions without you.
7. **Foresight** — learn from the past, understand the present, and anticipate the likely consequences of decisions. Foresight is what turns retrospectives into changed behavior.
8. **Stewardship** — hold the team, its resources, and its trust *in trust* for others: for the organization, the customers, and society. Act as if you must hand it all over in better shape than you got it.
9. **Commitment to the growth of people** — invest in every person's professional *and* personal growth: development goals, stretch work, coaching, and real career conversations — not just performance reviews.
10. **Building community** — create belonging: shared identity, psychological safety, and connection among people who work together, especially across locations and functions.

## Adapting to the person: the DISC extension

Servant leadership is a posture, not a script. The same attribute lands differently depending on the persona you're serving. Use DISC to tune the delivery.

### D — Dominance (direct, results-driven, decisive, impatient)

- **Listening/Empathy:** be brief and concrete; don't mistake bluntness for hostility. Let them state the goal before you probe the feelings.
- **Persuasion:** argue from results and speed — "this path ships two weeks earlier". Never pull rank; give them a worthy challenge instead.
- **Growth:** grow them with ownership — bigger scope, harder problems, visible stakes. Micro-management is the fastest way to lose a D.
- **Foresight/Stewardship:** channel their urgency; make the long-term cost of shortcuts explicit in results language (rework, slower future delivery).
- **Watch for:** them steamrolling S and C voices in meetings — your job is to make space for the quieter analysis.

### I — Influence (enthusiastic, social, optimistic, persuasive)

- **Listening:** give the conversation room; they think out loud. Summarize back to convert energy into commitments.
- **Persuasion:** they respond to vision, stories, and who else is on board. Co-create publicly.
- **Growth:** recognition matters — praise publicly and specifically. Give them people-facing roles: demos, onboarding, cross-team liaising.
- **Community/Healing:** they are your natural allies in building community and defusing conflict — deputize them.
- **Watch for:** commitments made in enthusiasm and dropped in follow-through; serve them with structure (written next steps, gentle deadlines) without dampening the energy.

### S — Steadiness (patient, loyal, supportive, change-averse)

- **Listening/Empathy:** they won't volunteer disagreement in a group; do it 1:1, ask directly, and wait through the pauses.
- **Healing:** they absorb team stress silently — check on them after conflicts and reorganizations even if they say "I'm fine".
- **Persuasion during change:** explain *why*, give time to adapt, show the stable ground (what does *not* change). Spring nothing on them.
- **Growth:** they under-ask for promotion and stretch roles; offer growth proactively, framed as helping the team.
- **Watch for:** overload — they say yes to everything. Stewardship here means protecting them from their own accommodation.

### C — Conscientiousness (analytical, precise, quality-focused, risk-averse)

- **Listening:** engage with the substance of their analysis; hand-waving destroys your credibility with a C.
- **Persuasion:** bring data, criteria, and trade-offs in writing, ideally in advance. "Because I said so" or "gut feel" doesn't land.
- **Growth:** grow them through mastery — deep expertise, quality ownership, mentoring others on rigor. Public spotlight is not a reward for most Cs.
- **Foresight/Conceptualization:** they excel at risk analysis — pull them into planning early; it serves them *and* the plan.
- **Watch for:** perfectionism blocking delivery — serve them by agreeing explicit "good enough" criteria (definition of done) so shipping isn't a personal quality failure.

## Giving feedback: STAR/AR

Structure every piece of behavioral feedback — positive or corrective — as:

- **ST — Situation/Task:** the specific context. "In yesterday's planning meeting, when we discussed the API migration…"
- **A — Action:** what the person actually did (observable behavior, not interpretation). "…you interrupted the tester twice and moved on before she finished."
- **R — Result:** the effect it had. "She stopped contributing, and we missed the regression risk she was raising."

For corrective feedback, add:

- **AA — Alternative Action:** what could be done instead. "Next time, ask her to finish the thought and note the concern on the board."
- **AR — Alternative Result:** the outcome the alternative would produce. "We'd have caught the risk in planning instead of in production, and she'd keep flagging issues early."

Rules of thumb: one behavior per conversation; deliver close to the event; describe behavior, never personality; positive STAR (without AA/AR) at least as often as corrective STAR/AR. Tune the delivery to DISC — direct and brief for D, warm and private for S, precise and evidence-based for C, energetic but written-down for I.
