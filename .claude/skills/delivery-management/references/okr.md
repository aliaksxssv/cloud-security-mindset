# Setting Goals with OKRs

## The 3×3 rule

For quarterly goals, the target is **3 Objectives, each with 3 Key Results**. Create them for the company first, then for each department (based on the company OKRs), then each team (based on the department OKRs), then each individual (based on the team OKRs).

> **Warning — the classic mistake:** creating department/team OKRs *without* company-level OKRs above them. This produces silo-ed fiefdoms that neither work together nor trust each other. Always cascade from the top.

## Objectives and Key Results

**The Objective (O)** answers: *"Where do we want to go?"*
- Should tell a compelling story — think of it as the tagline of a Hollywood movie.
- Does not need to be measurable (it can be subjective), but it must be **inspiring**.

**Key Results (KRs)** answer: *"How do we know we're getting there?"*
- Must be **objectively measurable** — defined clearly enough that a third party could adjudicate whether they were completed.
- A KR can be a metric, a step, or an action. Example:

> **Objective:** Massively grow revenues.
> - $500,000 MRR *(metric that tells us we've reached the result)*
> - Hire ten additional SDRs *(step needed to get to the result)*
> - Hire a Sales Ops person to project-manage the sales team *(action required to get there)*

Company OKRs should be broad enough that every department and team can see their work contributing to at least one. Typical company-level themes:
- **Profits:** massively grow revenues while minimizing expense
- **Product:** delight our customers
- **People:** create a positive, transparent environment where everyone is inspired to do their best work

## Forming OKRs

At the end of every quarter, gather the leadership team:

1. Each member **separately writes up** what they think the company OKRs should be.
2. **Combine** everyone's ideas into one document — common themes will emerge.
3. Have the **healthy debate** about priorities.
4. The **Decision-Maker chooses**. This is an exercise in hearing people out, not in consensus.

Then each department head runs the same process in their department, and down to individuals in one-on-ones. Each person should **draft their own OKRs** (aligned with the level above) rather than having them dictated — that's what creates buy-in.

Keep the whole process to **one week or less** ("Admin Week"), bundled with the other quarterly admin (last quarter's OKR debrief, performance management).

### Admin Week timeline

| Day | Activity |
|---|---|
| **Monday** | Leadership team (all department heads), 2h: create company OKRs via structured brainstorming |
| **Tuesday** | Each department, 2h group meeting: department OKRs. CEO checks and approves by end of day |
| **Wednesday** | Each team, 2h: team OKRs. Department head approves by end of day |
| **Thursday** | Each sub-team, 2h: sub-team OKRs. Team head approves by end of day |
| **Friday** | Individuals create individual OKRs. Sub-team head reviews and approves by end of day |
| **Monday** | Leadership meeting: CEO checks and approves all OKRs. Announce to the company at All Hands |

## Tracking OKRs

- Create a tracking system: a third-party tool (15Five, Betterworks) or a traffic-lighted Google Sheet / Notion page.
- Show **week by week** which OKRs are on track (**Green**), slightly off-track (**Yellow**), far off-track (**Red**).
- Update statuses as part of the one-on-one process, at every level of management.
- Every **Red** OKR requires its **DRI** (directly responsible individual) to write an **Issue/Solution** describing how to get back on track.
- This method works well under ~100 people; larger organizations need to tweak it (see Pinterest's "Pyramid OKRs" for one example).

## Quality checklist for a drafted OKR set

- [ ] Exactly ~3 Objectives, ~3 KRs each — more means nothing is a priority
- [ ] Each O is inspiring and directional; each KR is third-party adjudicable
- [ ] KRs are outcomes where possible (see `outcome-vs-output.md`), steps/actions only where a metric truly doesn't exist yet
- [ ] Every lower-level OKR traces to one level above
- [ ] Each KR has a DRI and a tracking cadence
